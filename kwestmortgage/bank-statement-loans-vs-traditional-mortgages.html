Self-Employed Borrowers

Bank Statement Loans vs. Traditional Mortgages for Self-Employed Borrowers

Self-employed borrowers often have strong cash flow, but traditional tax-return income may not always tell the full story. Here’s how bank statement loans compare with traditional mortgage options.

Two ways lenders can review self-employed income

For self-employed buyers in Key West and Monroe County, the way a lender measures income can change what loan amount you qualify for. Two common paths are traditional mortgages and bank statement loans, and each looks at income differently.

  • Traditional mortgages usually use W-2s, pay stubs, tax returns, and standard income calculations.
  • Bank statement loans may use 12 or 24 months of personal or business bank statements to review cash flow.
  • A bank statement loan may help when business deductions reduce taxable income, but it may cost more than a traditional mortgage.

Bank statement loans

Pros and cons

Pros
  • Flexible income review
  • May better reflect actual cash flow
  • Can help self-employed borrowers whose tax returns show lower net income
  • Useful for business owners, freelancers, consultants, real estate professionals, and commission-based borrowers
  • Can sometimes support a larger qualifying income than a traditional calculation
Cons
  • Usually higher rates
  • Potentially higher points or fees
  • Larger down payment may be required
  • Reserve requirements may be stronger
  • Underwriting varies more by lender
  • Not always the best choice if the borrower can qualify traditionally
Traditional mortgages

Pros and cons for self-employed borrowers

Pros
  • Usually lower rates
  • More standardized guidelines
  • More widely available
  • Easier to compare between lenders
  • May allow a lower down payment depending on the program
  • Often better long-term cost if the borrower qualifies
Cons
  • Heavy reliance on tax returns
  • Business deductions can reduce qualifying income
  • Irregular income may be harder to document
  • More paperwork
  • May not capture the borrower’s real cash-flow strength
Choosing a path

Which option is better?

A traditional mortgage is usually the first option to evaluate because it may offer lower costs. But if tax returns do not support the loan amount, a bank statement loan may provide an alternative path.

At K West Mortgage — through West Coast Capital Mortgage Inc. — we often start by comparing the traditional path first. If that does not fit the borrower’s income profile, we can review bank statement and Non-QM options.

Example scenario

A self-employed borrower has strong business deposits but uses legitimate deductions that lower taxable income. A traditional mortgage may calculate lower qualifying income.

A bank statement loan may evaluate deposits and cash flow differently — but the borrower should compare rate, payment, down payment, reserves, and long-term cost before choosing.

FAQ

Common questions

Are bank statement loans only for self-employed borrowers?

They are commonly used by self-employed borrowers, business owners, freelancers, and borrowers whose cash flow is stronger than their tax-return income.

Are bank statement loans more expensive than traditional mortgages?

Often, yes. Bank statement loans are typically Non-QM products and may have higher rates, fees, down payment, or reserve requirements.

How many months of bank statements are usually reviewed?

Many programs review 12 or 24 months of personal or business bank statements, but requirements vary by lender.

Is a traditional mortgage better if I qualify?

Often yes. If your documented income supports the loan, a traditional mortgage may offer lower costs and more standardized terms.

Can I qualify if my tax returns show lower income because of business deductions?

Possibly. A bank statement loan may allow a lender to evaluate deposits and cash flow instead of relying only on taxable income.
